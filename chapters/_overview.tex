This half of the guide is the fast map. It collects the snapshot card and the part summaries for all six weeks, with no detailed content in between, so the whole shape of the course can be scanned in a few pages. Each part summary ends with a link to the section in the detailed reference half where its bullets and prose live. The detailed chapters in turn link back to the matching week here.

% ===================== WEEK 1 =====================
\section*{Week 1 — Introduction to Research}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week1}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week1.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{conceptgreen}{Week 1 — Introduction to Research}{Course start, no prerequisites}{Conceptual and technical design (\autoref{ch:week2}), sampling (\autoref{ch:week3}), reports (\autoref{ch:week6})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The research process runs in six fixed steps: the problem, the sample, the design, measure, analyse, conclude.
  \item A hypothesis is a concrete prediction, and its form fixes whether the study is descriptive, relational, or causal.
  \item The variable is the smallest unit of research language, and its values are attributes that must be exhaustive and mutually exclusive.
  \item An observed relationship may be correlational, causal, or produced by a third variable, so correlation never proves causation.
  \item Deduction reasons from general to specific and induction from specific to general, and the hourglass joins the two in one study.
  \item Feasibility rather than interest decides whether an idea becomes a project, and the literature review sets that idea in context.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — The Research Process.} This part fixes the skeleton that every later week hangs on: the six-step pipeline from a broad problem to a defensible conclusion. It matters because each step constrains the next, so a weak sample or an unstated limitation propagates all the way to the conclusion. The design step is the hinge that Week~2 expands into conceptual and technical design (\autoref{ch:week2}), and the measure step is what Week~3 turns into sampling and data (\autoref{ch:week3}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:research-process}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — The Language of Research.} This part supplies the vocabulary that makes the rest of the course precise: hypothesis, study type, variable, attribute, and the kinds of relationship between variables. It matters because the form of the hypothesis decides the type of study, and the type of study decides what evidence will count. The closing warning, that correlation is not causation, is the single most reused idea in the guide and reappears in validation (\autoref{sec:vv}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:language-research}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 3 — The Hourglass: Deduction and Induction.} This part gives the shape of the reasoning that runs through a single study, narrowing from broad questions down to measurement and widening back out to general claims. It matters because the two directions of inference, deduction downward and induction upward, carry different risks, and induction is where most flawed conclusions are born. The operationalise step at the neck of the hourglass is exactly what Week~2 formalises (\autoref{sec:operationalization}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:hourglass}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 4 — Where Research Ideas Come From.} This part covers the front end of a project, how ideas arise, whether they are feasible, and how a literature review frames them. It matters because an interesting idea that fails on time, ethics, cost, or access never becomes a viable project, so feasibility is a gate rather than an afterthought. The literature review introduced here is what locates the gap that Week~2 turns into a research objective (\autoref{sec:objectives-rqs}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:research-ideas}.\end{partbox}

\section*{Week 2 — Research Design}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week2}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week2.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{conceptgreen}{Week 2 — Research Design}{Language of research, induction and deduction, steps of a research project (\autoref{ch:week1})}{Reference list (\autoref{ch:week6}), project management (\autoref{ch:week5})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Research design splits into conceptual design, which fixes what, why, and how much, and technical design, which fixes how, where, and when.
  \item Conceptual design has four phases: the objective, the research framework, the research question or conceptual model, and definition with operationalisation.
  \item A good research objective is informative, useful, feasible, and clear, and it names a contribution and the way that contribution is delivered.
  \item Research questions must be open-ended, number at least two, and be relevant, researchable, anchored, and precise.
  \item Technical design has three phases: research strategy, research material, and research planning.
  \item Reproducibility and the code of conduct govern conduct, and any work with human subjects requires HREC approval.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — Strategic Areas.} This part splits the single design step of Week~1 (\autoref{sec:research-process}) into two strategic areas that are tackled in order. Conceptual design models the content of the work and answers what, why, and how much, while technical design plans the execution and answers how, where, and when. The split matters because the technical decisions are meaningless until the conceptual ones are settled, so the order is a dependency rather than a preference. Full detail in \autoref{sec:conceptual-technical}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — Conceptual Design.} This part is the engine room of conceptual design, where the objective, the research questions, the framework, and the operationalisation are built. It matters because these four outputs are what the technical design consumes, and a weak research question set is the usual reason a technical design will not come together. The criteria and rules given here are the same ones collected as checklists in Appendix~\ref{ch:checklists}. Full detail in \autoref{sec:objectives-rqs}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 3 — Technical Design.} This part turns the operationalised concepts into an executable plan through three phases, the research strategy, the research materials, and the research planning. It matters because this is where feasibility stops being abstract and becomes a schedule with milestones, equipment, and contingencies. The strategy criteria here echo the feasibility gate of Week~1 (\autoref{sec:research-ideas}), and the planning tools named here are developed fully in Week~5 (\autoref{sec:project-mgmt}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:strategy-materials}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 4 — Ethics and Conduct.} This part sets the ethical baseline for the whole course, built on reproducibility, traceability, proper credit, and the code of conduct. It matters because these duties constrain every later choice about data and method, and because human-subject work is gated by a formal committee rather than left to judgement. The traceability requirement here is the same one reproducibility and data management depend on later (\autoref{sec:eop}, \autoref{sec:dmp}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:ethics}.\end{partbox}

\section*{Week 3 — Research Material and Data}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week3}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week3.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{empiricred}{Week 3 — Research Material and Data}{Steps of a research project (\autoref{ch:week1})}{Verification and validation, sustainable research (\autoref{ch:week4}), data management (\autoref{ch:week5})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The population is the entire collection in the field, and the sample is the representative subset that is actually studied.
  \item The sampling method narrows from the theoretical population, through the study population and the sampling frame, to the sample.
  \item Probability sampling supports generalisation to the population, while non-probability sampling never can and must say so explicitly.
  \item The standard error measures the precision of a sample, falling as the sample size grows and as the standard deviation shrinks.
  \item Data is qualitative or quantitative, and its type follows from the strategy, the materials, and the defined variables.
  \item Data quality must be checked before analysis, and a detailed research logbook is what makes the whole project traceable.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — Population, Sample, and the Sampling Method.} This part makes the sample step of Week~1 (\autoref{sec:research-process}) precise by separating the population from the sample and showing how one narrows to the other. It matters because representativeness is what licenses any statement about the population, and the inductive leap from sample to population is exactly the risk flagged in the hourglass (\autoref{sec:hourglass}). The four-stage funnel given here names where access and listing constraints quietly shrink the reachable population. Full detail in \autoref{sec:sampling}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — Types of Samples and Standard Error.} This part classifies sampling into probability and non-probability methods and introduces the standard error as the measure of a sample's precision. It matters because the probability boundary is the same boundary as generalisability, so the sampling choice decides in advance what the study can claim. The standard error then quantifies how tightly the sample estimates the population, with two levers that reappear in the resource questions of Week~4 (\autoref{sec:eop}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:sample-types}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 3 — Data Sources, Quality, and the Logbook.} This part covers where data comes from, the legal categories that constrain it, the qualitative and quantitative split, and the quality checks and logbook that protect it. It matters because the data rules are hard gates, GDPR for personal data and a knowledge embargo for protected fields, that can stop a project before it starts. The logbook introduced here is the practical instrument behind the traceability demanded by ethics (\autoref{sec:ethics}) and the full data management plan of Week~5 (\autoref{sec:dmp}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:data-sources}.\end{partbox}

\section*{Week 4 — Validation and Sustainability}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week4}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week4.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{empiricred}{Week 4 — Validation and Sustainability}{Sampling and data (\autoref{ch:week3})}{Data management and project management (\autoref{ch:week5})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Verification proves the method was used correctly and suits the topic, while validation proves the outcomes are true on strong evidence.
  \item Both are needed for credibility, professional use, and reproducibility, and both must be designed into the plan with time reserved.
  \item In industry, verification is internal quality control against specifications and validation is external quality assurance of fitness for purpose.
  \item The Engineering for One Planet framework weighs research on its value, its reproducibility, and its resource management.
  \item Reproducibility depends on good data management and is blocked by complexity, technology change, human error, and intellectual property.
  \item Resource management spans digital habits and methodology choices, each judged on whether the cost matches the accuracy actually needed.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — Verification and Validation.} This part draws the line between proving a method was used correctly and proving the result is true, the two distinct guarantees a credible study must give. It matters because a method can be executed flawlessly and still yield a false conclusion, which is the correlation-and-causation problem of Week~1 wearing a different name (\autoref{sec:language-research}). Both checks consume time and must be planned for, linking straight back to research planning (\autoref{sec:strategy-materials}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:vv}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — Engineering for One Planet.} This part introduces the Engineering for One Planet framework, which judges research on its value, its reproducibility, and its resource use. It matters because it reframes sustainability as a design constraint that touches topic choice, documentation, and method, rather than an ethical extra bolted on at the end. The reproducibility axis depends on the data management of Week~5 (\autoref{sec:dmp}), and the resource axis gives weight to the sample-size and accuracy trade-off from Week~3 (\autoref{sec:sample-types}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:eop}.\end{partbox}

\section*{Week 5 — Data Management and Project Planning}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week5}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week5.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{integblue}{Week 5 — Data Management and Project Planning}{Technical design (\autoref{ch:week2}), sustainable research and data (\autoref{ch:week3}, \autoref{ch:week4})}{Reporting (\autoref{ch:week6})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A data management plan has six sections, from data description to responsibilities, and must be designed before any data is collected.
  \item FAIR data is findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable, which is the goal that good documentation and metadata serve.
  \item The 3-2-1 rule keeps three copies on two media with one off-site, fixing the backup strategy from the start.
  \item Project management gives a research project structured timelines, optimised resources, early risk detection, and clear communication.
  \item A Work Breakdown Structure divides the project into work packages and tasks down to a level where each task can be estimated.
  \item A Gantt chart schedules the work and exposes the critical path, the chain that sets the shortest possible project duration.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — The Data Management Plan.} This part turns the logbook habit of Week~3 (\autoref{sec:data-sources}) into a designed system, the six-section data management plan. It matters because the plan must exist before data collection, since data cannot be organised retrospectively, and because it is the concrete form of the reproducibility duty from Week~4 (\autoref{sec:eop}). The plan is not a deliverable for this course, but its structure is examinable. Full detail in \autoref{sec:dmp}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — Project Management in Research.} This part develops the planning tools named in Week~2 (\autoref{sec:strategy-materials}) into full project management, from task estimation through the Work Breakdown Structure to the Gantt chart. It matters because a schedule is only trustworthy once the work is decomposed to estimable pieces and the critical path is known. The same precondition recurs here as in Week~2, that planning is possible only after both designs are settled. Full detail in \autoref{sec:project-mgmt}.\end{partbox}

\section*{Week 6 — Reporting and Writing}
\phantomsection\label{ov:week6}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{week6.png}
\end{figure}

\begin{snapcard}{integblue}{Week 6 — Reporting and Writing}{Steps of a research project (\autoref{ch:week1}), conceptual design (\autoref{ch:week2})}{The Research Methodologies report (\autoref{ch:reportguide})}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A technical report conveys specific information about a technical subject to a specific audience for a specific and practical purpose.
  \item The Research Methodologies report conveys a research plan to a technical peer in order to organise the research activities.
  \item Every report has a head that introduces, a main body of connected sections, and feet that conclude without adding new information.
  \item Choose one reference style and keep it, either author-date such as APA or numbered such as AIAA, each with its own trade-off.
  \item A reference list should use at least three document types, lean on peer-reviewed articles, and stay mostly under ten years old.
  \item Reader-centred writing guides the reader in through titles, figures, and conclusions first, and a final checklist closes the report.
\end{itemize}
\end{snapcard}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 1 — Technical Reports.} This part defines what a technical report is and maps the generic report skeleton onto the Research Methodologies report specifically. It matters because the report is where the conceptual and empirical tracks of the whole course are finally written down, and its structure mirrors the work done in Weeks~2 and~5. The section-by-section mapping is developed in full in the report guide (\autoref{ch:reportguide}). Full detail in \autoref{sec:reports}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 2 — Referencing.} This part sets the rules for sourcing and for the two accepted citation styles, author-date and numbered. It matters because references are where the give-credit duty of Week~2 ethics (\autoref{sec:ethics}) becomes a concrete, checkable practice, and because mixing styles is a common avoidable error. The two styles trade in-text information against readability in opposite ways. Full detail in \autoref{sec:referencing}.\end{partbox}

\begin{partbox}\textbf{Part 3 — Writing Style.} This part collects the style guidance, from defining the target reader, through graph conventions and the responsible use of AI tools, to the final writing checklist. It matters because reader-centred choices about titles, figures, and layout are what get a report read at all, and the figure conventions here extend the data presentation of Week~3. The closing checklist is the last gate before a report is considered finished. Full detail in \autoref{sec:writing-style}.\end{partbox}
